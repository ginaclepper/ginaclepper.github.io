Gina Clepper's Assignment 4!

Assignment: Design and mill a box out of FR1 on the Othermill, using boxjoints, tabs, or another joining method. (Alternatively, 3D print a similar box.)


For this assignment, I elected to use the Othermill because I wanted to practice using the milling machine. I decided to make the simplest box I could think of that functioned as described.


Measurements

I used calipers to measure several pieces of the available FR1. 1.54 mm seemed to be the most common depth, though there was some variance from board to board (no more than 0.1 mm, from the boards I measured.)


Design

I drafted a simple puzzle-like box side in Rhino, in the following steps:

  1. Create a square.
  2. Create rectangles for tabs on one edge of the square, then create rectangles for corresponding slots on the opposite edge. Use Trim to remove extraneous paths. Repeat for the remaining pair of opposite edges of the square. How wide the tabs are can vary, but their depth should be the estimated thickness of the material (in this case, 1.54 mm.)
  3. To leave slight room for joining pieces, use Offset to offset the slots to be 0.1 mm larger.
  4. Finally, at each acute angle in the path (acute relative to the outside of the object, the direction from which the mill will do its milling), Trim out a "dogbone," a circular cutout. I used circles 1mm in diameter, the width of the end mill I would be using.


I partially modeled what the box would look like assembled using an extruded version of the piece. (But only partially modeling failed to bring to my attention the need for special top and bottom pieces with this design, as I would realize later!)


Preparing to mill

I used the Rhino to KiCAD (as DXF) to Bantam Tools (as gerber) workflow. Over the course of many attempts I learned the following:

  • I need to export from Rhino as a DXF with a "lines" scheme for it to show up as having graphic content in KiCAD.
  • KiCAD doesn't like overlapping paths -- and this might include hidden layers that contain overlapping paths. (More investigation needed!)
  • The Bantam software doesn't seem to like a blank gerber file for engraving. I worked around this, but I'm certain there's a better way!
  • The dogbones I used triggered a warning about needing a smaller tool. I ignored this warning without issue, because my interest was in milling out those corners, not perfectly producing those circles, but I'm not sure if I should have. The red areas are those highlighted as possibly problematic:
  • When laying out a design in the Bantam software, the origin is in the front left corner!


Milling

I applied as much double stick tape as I could without overlapping pieces to the bottom of my FR1 and to the bottom of the sacrificial layer of FR1. I did not measure the sandwiched FR1 and tape (mistake) but assumed a Z offset of 2mm, a distance that would ensure the sacrificial later would be cut into but not so deep as to reach the metal bed.
